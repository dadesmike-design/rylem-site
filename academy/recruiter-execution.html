📞 The Candidate Screen Call (MASTER THIS)
💡
Before Every Call

Pull up the job in CEIPAL. Pull up the candidate's resume AND LinkedIn. Prepare your screening questions based on the JD.

1️⃣Opening & Intro▼
  • Use candidate's first AND last name
  • Identify yourself as Rylem Staffing
  • State the goal: "I have a client and role that would be a great fit with your experience, but first I'd like to learn about you."
  • Set context: "I'm going to ask you lots of questions about your experience, but this call is mainly to learn about you, what you're looking for, and see if I have an opportunity to fit your needs."
  • Ask: "Could you tell me about yourself and why you're looking?"
  • LET THE CANDIDATE TALK. Do not interrupt. Listen.
2️⃣Present the Role▼
  • Connect to what they shared: "Based on what you told me about what you're looking for, my client [Company] is hiring for a [Role] and I think this would be a great fit."
  • Share selling points about the company — don't just recite the JD
  • Describe the role in your own words (not copy-pasted from the job description)
3️⃣Screening Questions▼
  • Ask the questions you prepped from the JD
  • Technical questions relevant to the role
  • Behavioral questions (STAR method examples)
  • DO NOT accept surface-level "yes" answers — always ask follow-ups to validate experience
⚠️
Example Follow-Up

"You said you have React experience — tell me about a project where you built something complex with it. What was the architecture? How big was the team?"

🔬Project Depth Probing — Scalability, Reliability & Complexity▼
💡
Why This Matters

Anyone can list technologies on a resume. These questions separate candidates who contributed from those who owned. Clients pay Rylem premiums for talent that's operated at scale — prove it on the screen call.

📈 Scalability Questions

Find out if they've built things that actually had to handle growth.

  • "How many users/transactions/records did that system handle daily? What about at peak?"
  • "Did you ever have to redesign something because it couldn't keep up with growth? Walk me through what happened."
  • "What was the data volume you were working with? Thousands of rows or millions?"
  • "Were you building for 10 users or 10,000? How did that affect your design decisions?"
  • "Tell me about a time the system hit a scaling wall. What broke first and how did you fix it?"
  • "Did you work with distributed systems, microservices, or was it a single application? Why was it set up that way?"
🛡️ Reliability Questions

Find out if they've kept things running when it mattered.

  • "What happened when something went down in production? What was your role in fixing it?"
  • "Did your team have SLAs or uptime targets? What were they and how did you meet them?"
  • "Walk me through your worst production incident. What caused it, what did you do, and what changed after?"
  • "How did you test before deploying? Was there a QA process, automated testing, staging environment?"
  • "Were you on-call? What did that look like — how often did things break at 2 AM?"
  • "What monitoring or alerting did you have in place? Did you set it up or was it already there?"
  • "How did you handle rollbacks when a release went sideways?"
🧩 Complexity Questions

Find out if they've solved hard problems or just followed instructions.

  • "What was the most technically challenging part of that project? Why was it hard?"
  • "How many systems or teams did your project touch? How did you coordinate across them?"
  • "Were you working with legacy code or building from scratch? Which is harder and why?"
  • "Tell me about a decision you made where there was no obvious right answer. How did you pick your approach?"
  • "How many people were on the project? What was your specific piece versus what others owned?"
  • "Did you have to learn a new technology or domain to deliver? How did you ramp up?"
  • "What tradeoffs did you have to make? Speed vs. quality, cost vs. features — give me a real example."
  • "If you had to do that project over again, what would you change?"
⚠️
Red Flags to Listen For
  • Vague numbers: "It was a big system" — push for specifics. How big?
  • "We" without "I": If every answer is what the team did, ask what THEY specifically owned
  • Can't explain why: They used Kubernetes but can't say why — they were along for the ride
  • No failures: Everyone has production war stories. No stories = no real ownership
  • Buzzword stacking: Lists 15 technologies but can't go deep on any of them
🎯Candidate Deep-Dive — 7 Categories Every Screen Must Cover▼
💡
Use These on Every Screen

These seven categories give you a complete picture of any candidate — technical or not. You don't need to ask every single question, but you need to touch every category before submitting anyone.

💼 1. Current Role & Project

Understand what they're actually doing right now — not what their resume says from 2 years ago.

  • "Walk me through a typical day in your current role."
  • "What project are you working on right now? What's the goal and where is it at?"
  • "What's your specific responsibility on this project versus the rest of the team?"
  • "Who do you report to? How big is the team around you?"
  • "How long have you been in this role and how has it changed since you started?"
  • "What's the most impactful thing you've delivered in the last 6 months?"
⚙️ 2. Technical Stack

Pin down what they actually use daily vs. what they touched once in a tutorial.

  • "What languages, frameworks, or platforms are you using every day right now?"
  • "What's your primary tool — the one you'd be lost without?"
  • "Which of the technologies on your resume have you used in the last 12 months?"
  • "Are you working in the cloud? Which one — AWS, Azure, GCP? What services specifically?"
  • "What databases are you working with? How are you querying them — raw SQL, ORM, something else?"
  • "What's on your resume that you'd rather NOT work with again? Why?"
🔧 3. Hands-On Depth

Separate the doers from the delegators and the managers who stopped coding 5 years ago.

  • "Are you writing code/building/configuring every day, or is your role more oversight and planning?"
  • "When was the last time you personally debugged a production issue? Walk me through it."
  • "Do you do code reviews? Give me an example of something you caught that would have been a problem."
  • "If I put you in front of a blank IDE on day one, could you start building — or do you need ramp time on the tools?"
  • "What percentage of your week is hands-on technical work vs. meetings, planning, and documentation?"
🏗️ 4. Architecture & Problem Solving

Can they think beyond their assigned task? Clients pay top dollar for people who see the big picture.

  • "Have you ever designed a system or solution from scratch? Tell me about it."
  • "When you get a new requirement, what's your process before you start building?"
  • "Tell me about a time you had to choose between two approaches. How did you decide?"
  • "Have you ever pushed back on a bad technical decision? What happened?"
  • "How do you handle a situation where the requirements are vague or keep changing?"
  • "What's a technical mistake you made that taught you something important?"
🖥️ 5. Environment & Tools

Match their workflow to the client's setup — mismatches here cause early-assignment friction.

  • "What does your dev/work environment look like — Mac, Windows, Linux?"
  • "What project management tools do you use? Jira, Azure DevOps, Asana, Monday?"
  • "Are you using CI/CD pipelines? Which ones — Jenkins, GitHub Actions, Azure Pipelines?"
  • "How does your team handle version control? Git flow, trunk-based, something else?"
  • "Agile, Scrum, Kanban, Waterfall — what methodology is your current team running?"
  • "How do you document your work? Do you write it up or is it tribal knowledge?"
🤝 6. Team & Collaboration

Technical skills get them in the door. Collaboration skills keep them on the assignment.

  • "Tell me about a disagreement with a teammate or stakeholder. How did you handle it?"
  • "Do you prefer working independently or as part of a tight-knit team? Why?"
  • "How do you handle getting pulled into a project mid-stream with no context?"
  • "Have you mentored junior team members? What did that look like?"
  • "How do you communicate progress — standups, Slack updates, written status reports?"
  • "What kind of manager do you work best with — hands-on or hands-off?"
🧭 7. Logistics & Motivation

The deal-breakers. Cover these BEFORE you invest time writing them up for a BDM.

  • "What's driving your search right now — why are you looking?"
  • "Are you open to contract, or only looking for full-time? What about contract-to-hire?"
  • "What's your availability? When could you start?"
  • "Are you interviewing anywhere else right now? How far along?"
  • "This role is [remote/hybrid/onsite] — does that work for you?"
  • "What would make you turn down an offer — even if the money was right?"
  • "Is there anything about your situation I should know — visa, non-compete, notice period?"
⚠️
Don't Skip Categories

It's tempting to go deep on technical and skip logistics. That's how you get a perfect candidate who can't start for 90 days or won't do contract work. Touch all 7 before you submit.

4️⃣Compensation▼
  • Ask: "What hourly rate or salary are you targeting?"
  • NEVER reveal the top of the pay range — let them tell you their number first
  • If their number is way off, handle it: "The market for this role is typically in the $X-Y range. Would that work for you?"
5️⃣Counter Offer Handling▼

If currently employed, proactively raise it: "What are you going to do when your company gives you a counter offer?"

  • "Given the market, they might offer you a counter."
  • "The reason you want to leave is still going to be there — it won't change."
  • "We've seen tons of times where someone accepts a counter and then their company starts looking for their replacement because they've already tried to leave."
6️⃣Next Steps▼
  • Tell them exactly when you'll contact them next
  • Explain the interview process and what to expect
  • Set clear expectations before ending the call

📄 Resume Review — Before Submitting
  • Read the ENTIRE resume against the JD and BDM's notes from the hiring manager
  • Write a comparison — don't just forward the resume
  • Check for employment gaps (address in write-up)
  • If not currently working → include reason why
  • If hybrid/onsite → verify they live close enough or will commute/relocate
  • Check formatting and spelling errors
  • Experience level match (don't submit a VP for a mid-level role)
  • Listen to your own screening recording for communication skills and culture fit
  • Ask yourself follow-up questions before submitting — call the candidate again if needed
🤝 Working with BDMs
  • BDM wins the job → hands off to you
  • Stay involved: attend intake calls with the HM
  • All submissions go through the BDM to the client — never go around them
  • BDM follows up with client next day after submission — they need detailed write-ups from you
  • Feedback loop is critical: if client rejects, get detailed reasons from BDM and adjust your search
  • Communicate status daily: where are your candidates in the pipeline?
